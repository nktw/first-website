Biography

Born in late colonial-era Hong Kong, I was raised by UK-educated parents who instilled in me an unique set of Chinese-western cultural values.

Images taken with Olympus XA2, my hobby camera

I studied in catholic schools throughout childhood and teenage years, and moved to England to pursue higher education in 2011.

I enrolled in a bachelor's degree program in Media Practice at the University of Sussex in Brighton. After one semester of gaining experience in making videos and other media, I switched to a program in Human Geography, from which I believed I would gain a wider understanding of the world around me.

During the first summer break from university, I took the opportunity to volunteer for a dolphin research institute in Sardinia, Italy. The experience inspired me to return home the following summer, and to work for a local environmental NGO while conducting a study in preparation for my thesis.

In 2016, I submitted my thesis and graduated with 1st Class Honor.

I gained my first professional experience working as an intern for an ESG research company based in London. The two-month long internship introduced me into the new world of ESG investing and soon afterwards I was employed as a full-time Chinese analyst at RepRisk.

In January 2016, I moved to Berlin, Germany, and became one of the first employees of RepRisk's Berlin office.

Four years on, I have become a full-fledged analyst and learnt a great deal of socially and environmentally sustainable business conducts.

Currently exploring new opportunities in multiple directions including journalism, urban sustainability, etc.

Arts of the Working Class: an art newspaper for all

Natalie Kwan
DECEMBER 10, 2019

It was a busy week for Berlin-based artist Paul Sochacki. Within five days, he helped to launch a new website and the newest issue of the Arts of the Working Class (AWC), an art newspaper launched in 2018 and partially distributed by the homeless in Berlin.

According to the latest estimation by the Federal Association for Homeless Assistance (BAG Wohnungslosenhilfe), there are 650,000 homeless people across Germany. It is believed that up to 10,000 of them are living on the streets of the German capital, while the number of Berliners considered to be without a permanent home is estimated at 50,000.

From the moment the idea of publishing an art newspaper sprung up, Sochacki has intended that the AWC would be distributed as a street newspaper. For him, the newspaper should serve the society as “a tool of solidarity that can basically work in any area where… [there is] high income inequality and people can pick it up and counter this inequality actively.”

The AWC operates on an advertising-supported revenue model, which means that the publishing and running costs are covered by advertisements. For each 30,000 printed copies of an issue, a majority are given for free to homeless people in Berlin and London for distribution and they retain all proceeds generated from the street sales. The rest are sold at different distribution points in cities such as Brussels, Madrid and Vienna.

Ecuador-born curator and co-founder of the AWC María Inés Plaza Lazo explained the title in an event in New York recently, “we are referring to a pre-industrial idea of labor… at the same time, we mock the idea that in our time there is a specific ideological frame that we can understand class struggle, since in our current governments there are no clear orientations and privatizing interests are moving the political sphere.”

Each issue of the AWC contains a carefully-curated selection of photographs, artworks, poems, commentaries, and articles written in different languages. Artists and writers also participate in the design of advertisements placed in the issue. For the AWC editorial team, the multilingualism of the newspaper allows for difference in cultural perspectives. They want the newspaper to be accessible, collaborative, and “an offer for everyone”, as emphasized by Alina Kolar, one of three AWC publishers, at the website launch event in Berlin.

Through these core elements, the AWC aims to redistribute power and wealth both within the art world and the society. Over the past 1.5 year, the newspaper has grown bigger in terms of content and outreach. For each new issue launched, the number of copies printed increases as they are being distributed by more street vendors and retail points.

The schedule of Sochacki in 2020 is already packed with planned launch of issues in new cities. While the budget of publishing the newspaper remains limited, it is for him and the rest of the AWC team a priority to find funding through advertising and a continuous struggle to maintain equilibrium that allows them to sustain their editorial office in a time of growth.
